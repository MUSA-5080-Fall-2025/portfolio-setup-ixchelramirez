Assignment Overview

Scenario

You are a data analyst for the California Department of Human Services. The department is considering implementing an algorithmic system to identify communities that should receive priority for social service funding and outreach programs. Your supervisor has asked you to evaluate the quality and reliability of available census data to inform this decision.

Drawing on our Week 2 discussion of algorithmic bias, you need to assess not just what the data shows, but how reliable it is and what communities might be affected by data quality issues.

2.2 Data Quality Assessment

Your Task: Calculate margin of error percentages and create reliability categories.

Requirements: - Calculate MOE percentage: (margin of error / estimate) * 100 - Create reliability categories: - High Confidence: MOE < 5% - Moderate Confidence: MOE 5-10%
- Low Confidence: MOE > 10% - Create a flag for unreliable estimates (MOE > 10%)

Hint: Use mutate() with case_when() for the categories.

# Calculate MOE percentage and reliability categories using mutate()
my_state <- my_state %>%
mutate(
MOE_PCT = (MedianHouseholdIncomeM / MedianHouseholdIncomeE) * 100,
reliability = case_when(
MOE_PCT < 5 ~ "High Confidence",
MOE_PCT >= 5 & MOE_PCT <= 10 ~ "Moderate Confidence",
MOE_PCT > 10 ~ "Low Confidence"
),
unreliable = MOE_PCT > 10
)

# Create a summary showing count of counties in each reliability category
# Hint: use count() and mutate() to add percentages

summary_reliability <- my_state %>%
  count(reliability) %>%
  mutate(percent = (n / sum(n)) * 100
)

Part 5: Policy Recommendations

5.1 Analysis Integration and Professional Summary

Executive Summary:

This analysis evaluated the reliability of American Community Survey (ACS) data for California counties and census tracts to inform the Department’s potential use of algorithmic systems in allocating social service funding. According to the state analysis, the majority of the counties (72%) have a high confidence of reliability with margins of error (MOE) below 5%. There are only 5 counties (9%) with low confidence of reliability as defined as having MOE above 10%. These 5 counties are Mono, Alpine, Sierra, Trinity and Plumas and indicate greater uncertainty in income estimates in rural locations. On the tract level all the tracts had low confidence because at least one of the racial demographic groups had a MOE above 10%. From an equity perspective, these data quality issues pose a serious risk of algorithmic bias. High MOE is frequently present in rural, sparsely populated, or demographically diverse areas, which means these communities are more likely to be flagged as unreliable. This could lead to their exclusion or underrepresentation in automated decision-making. The data for California follows this pattern as Mono, Alpine, Sierra and Trinity are within the top five least populous counties in California. Trinity, which is the 5th least populous county, does not have an incorporated city which could make it hard to collect demographic population as the population is even more dispersed. In contrast, larger urban counties, such as Los Angeles, benefit from lower MOEs and thus are more likely prioritized in algorithmic decisions. This risks reinforcing existing disparities between larger metropolitan regions and rural communities with regards to allocation of resources and services due to the limitations of reliable data.

The underlying factors that cause these issues stem from the ACS’s sampling design. The ACS relies on sampling, thus counties and tracts with smaller populations generate less precise estimates since fewer households are surveyed which inflates margins of error. Demographic subgroups such as Black and Hispanic residents are particularly affected at the tract level, where margins of error often exceed 15%. As a result, structural features of the survey produce uneven levels of statistical confidence across communities. If left unaddressed, these disparities can directly translate into biased outcomes when using data in automated systems.

To mitigate these risks, the Department should adopt a tiered oversight approach. In high confidence counties, algorithmic use is appropriate if outputs are reviewed on a regular basis. In moderate confidence areas, algorithmic outputs can be paired with human review to ensure accuracy and fairness. In low confidence areas, decisions should not be automated; instead, an expert can guide decisions, or the data can be supplemented with additional administrative data. Across all confidence levels, the Department should implement equity audits, establish transparent documentation of MOE thresholds, and cross-validate ACS estimates with state and local records. This combination of algorithmic efficiency and structured oversight would enable the Department to take advantage of automation while safeguarding equitable treatment across California communities.
